## What Is Position Sizing?

 Position sizing is the process of deciding how many units of a security to buy or sell. It is an important part of an investment strategy because it impacts both risk management and the maximization of potential returns. An investor's account size and risk tolerance should be considered when determining position size. This is particularly relevant for day traders, [forex](https://www.investopedia.com/best-brokers-for-forex-trading-4587882) traders, and individual investors seeking to balance risk and reward effectively.

## Position Sizing Basics for Investors

 Position sizing refers to the size of a position within a particular portfolio, or the dollar amount an investor plans to trade. Investors use position sizing to [align trade size](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/trading/09/determine-position-size.asp) with their risk tolerance and portfolio objectives.

 Position sizing is crucial in many investment types, but it's most often linked with day trading and [forex](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/forex.asp).

## Practical Example of Position Sizing

 Determining an [appropriate position size](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/trading/09/determine-position-size.asp) typically involves three steps:

### Understanding Account Risk

 Before using correct position sizing for a trade, investors must first figure out their account risk. This typically gets expressed as a percentage of the investor’s capital. As a rule of thumb, most retail investors [risk no more than 2%](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/two-percent-rule.asp) of their investment capital on any one trade; fund managers usually risk less than this amount.

For example, if an investor has a $25,000 account and sets their maximum account risk to 2%, they cannot risk more than $500 per trade (2% x $25,000). Even if the investor loses 10 consecutive trades in a row, they have only lost 20% of their investment capital.

### Assessing Trade Risk

 The investor must then determine where to place their [stop-loss order](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stop-lossorder.asp) for the specific trade. For stock trades, the [trade risk](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketrisk.asp) is the dollar difference between the entry price and the stop-loss. For example, if an investor intends to purchase Apple Inc. at $160 and place a stop-loss order at $140, the trade risk is $20 per share.

### Determining the Right Position Size

 The investor now knows they can risk $500 per trade and $20 per share. To determine the correct position size from this information, the investor simply needs to divide the account risk ($500) by the trade risk ($20). This means 25 [shares](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/shares.asp) can be bought ($500 / $20).

## How to Manage Gap Risk with Position Sizing

 Even with proper position sizing, investors can lose more than their intended risk amount if a security [gaps](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/gaprisk.asp) beyond a stop-loss order.

If expecting volatility, like before earnings reports, investors might halve their position size to cut gap risk.

## The Bottom Line

 Position sizing is essential for [managing risk and optimizing returns](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/risk.asp) by aligning trade size with account size and risk tolerance. Investors should factor in account and trade risk to set effective stop-loss orders and avoid heavy losses, while staying alert to gap risk during volatile periods. Regularly reassessing position sizing ensures it remains aligned with evolving goals and market conditions.
